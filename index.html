Understanding Your Results

BMI is a standardized method used by healthcare providers to assess weight-related health risks. It categorizes individuals into Underweight, Healthy Weight, Overweight, and Obese based on the density of mass relative to height.

While useful, it is important to note that BMI does not distinguish between muscle and fat. Highly active individuals or athletes may receive an "Overweight" score due to high muscle density rather than high body fat.

Health Implications

  • Underweight: May indicate nutritional deficiencies or underlying health issues.
  • Healthy Weight: Lower risk of chronic diseases like Type 2 diabetes and heart disease.
  • Overweight/Obese: Associated with higher risks of hypertension and cardiovascular strain.
Note: Ethnicities, particularly Asian and South Asian populations, may face higher health risks at lower BMI levels than the standard international cut-offs.

Plan Your Finances

An EMI (Equated Monthly Installment) allows you to pay back a large loan in manageable chunks over a set period. Each payment consists of both the principal amount and the interest charged by the bank.

Interest vs. Principal

In the early stages of your loan, a larger portion of your EMI goes toward paying the interest. As time progresses and the outstanding principal decreases, the interest component reduces, and more of your payment goes toward clearing the actual loan amount.

Pro Tip: Making small prepayments towards your principal can significantly reduce the total interest paid and shorten your loan tenure.

Ideal Body Weight (IBW)

What is "Ideal"?

Ideal Body Weight represents a weight range statistically associated with the lowest mortality rate and best health outcomes for your specific height.

Medical professionals use different standardized calculations to find this number. While these serve as excellent targets for weight loss or gain, they are general guidelines. Your frame size (small, medium, or large) and muscle mass will shift your personal ideal weight higher or lower.

Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)

The Science of Metabolism

Your BMR is the energy required to keep your body functioning while at rest. Even when sleeping, your body burns energy to pump blood, breathe, and regulate hormones.

BMR accounts for approximately 60-75% of the total calories you burn in a day. Generally, the more muscle mass you have, the higher your BMR will be, as muscle tissue requires more energy to maintain than fat tissue.

TDEE (Daily Calorie Burn)

Total Daily Energy Expenditure

TDEE is the most important number for weight management. It combines your BMR with your daily physical activity. To lose weight, you must consume fewer calories than your TDEE; to gain weight, you must consume more.

This estimation includes the "Thermic Effect of Food" (calories burned digesting) and "Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis" (calories burned walking, fidgeting, and moving throughout the day).
